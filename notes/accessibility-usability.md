# Accessibility and Usability

## Purpose

A digital humanities archive should be usable by public visitors, researchers, students, and future maintainers. Accessibility and usability are not separate from implementation; they affect navigation, metadata labels, layout, search behavior, and content maintenance.

## Usability Goals

- Make the archive purpose clear quickly.
- Help users find search and browse paths.
- Keep record pages readable and scannable.
- Use consistent labels across navigation and metadata.
- Support mobile and desktop layouts.
- Make documents and related records easy to discover.
- Reduce confusion for future maintainers adding new content.

## Accessibility Considerations

- Clear heading structure.
- Readable text size and contrast.
- Descriptive link text.
- Keyboard-friendly navigation where possible.
- Labels for search and navigation controls.
- Responsive layouts that do not hide important content.
- Avoiding image-only communication for important information.

## Metadata And Findability

Accessibility also includes findability. If records are poorly labeled or inconsistently organized, users may not be able to understand or locate archive materials. Metadata structure, naming conventions, and browse categories all affect the user's ability to make sense of the archive.

## Maintainer Usability

For client-managed archives, usability also applies behind the scenes. Future maintainers need to understand how to add records, update pages, manage documents, and preserve the structure over time.

## What This Demonstrates

- Accessibility-aware UX thinking.
- Responsive web design priorities.
- Public archive usability.
- Metadata organization for findability.
- Long-term support and maintainability.
